Our Story

Three generations
of patience.

When Elena Marchetti planted the first Pinot Noir rootstock on this hillside in 2008, she wasn't building a brand. She was making a promise to the earth beneath her feet: that nothing would leave this land until it was ready to speak for itself. Her grandmother had tended vines in Piedmont with the same quiet conviction, believing that great wine is not made — it is waited for.

Today, Noma remains fiercely small. Forty-two acres of volcanic loam and ancient seabed, tilted just so toward the afternoon fog that rolls through the Petaluma Gap. Every vintage is a conversation between the winemaker and the soil, guided by instinct, restrained by discipline. We ferment with native yeasts. We age in French oak from the Allier forests. We bottle only what moves us.

There are no shortcuts here. Each bottle carries the weight of seasons, the memory of rain, the patience of years spent in darkness. This is wine that remembers where it came from.

The Estate

The Land.

Our forty-two acres occupy a singular crease in the western Sonoma hillside where volcanic soils meet ancient marine sediment — a geological seam that gives our wines their distinctive mineral backbone and aromatic complexity. The vines are planted at elevations between 400 and 800 feet, capturing the cool maritime influence of the Pacific while sheltering from its harshest winds.

The morning fog arrives like clockwork, slowing ripening and preserving the natural acidity that gives our wines their tension and longevity. By afternoon, the sun warms the fractured basalt and sandstone, coaxing depth and concentration from every cluster. It is a place of contrasts — warm days and cold nights, ancient rock and young vines, wild silence and patient labor.
